# Remarks concerning the \(O(Za^2)\) corrections to Fermi decays, conserved-vector-current predictions, and universality

A. Sirlin

*Physical Review D*, 1 June 1987

Department of Physics, New York University, New York, New York 10003
(Received 2 December 1986; revised manuscript received 18 February 1987)

---

## Abstract

This article studies finite-nuclear-size contributions to the \(O(Za^2)\) corrections to Fermi decays using realistic nuclear-charge distributions. Combined with results from Koslowsky et al. and recent work by the author with Zucchini and by Jaus and Rasche, these refinements yield an average \(\mathcal{F}_t = 3070.6 \pm 1.6\) s for accurately measured superallowed Fermi transitions. Correspondingly, \(V_{ud} = 0.9744 \pm 0.0010\) and \(V_{ud}^2 + V_{us}^2 + V_{ub}^2 = 0.9979 \pm 0.0021\), in good agreement with the three-generation standard model at the level of its quantum corrections. Agreement with conserved-vector-current (CVC) predictions is very good, with each of the eight transitions differing from the average by \(< 1\sigma\). The consequences of using two other calculations of the nuclear mismatch correction \(\delta_c\) (Wilkinson's microscopic analysis and the recent results of Ormand and Brown) are briefly discussed. A useful upper bound on \(\mathcal{F}_t\), independent of the \(\delta_c\) calculation, is given.

---

## 1. Introduction and Background

After a 14-year hiatus, significant progress has been achieved in the analysis of the \(O(Za^2)\) corrections to Fermi decays, bringing highly accurate experiments into close agreement with CVC predictions and the standard model (SM) at the level of quantum corrections.\(^{1,2}\)

The \(O(Za^2)\) corrections are defined as contributions of this order to the positron-nucleus interaction not contained in the product \(F(Z,E)(1 + \delta_1)\), where \(F(Z,E)\) is the Fermi function and \(\delta_1\) the \(O(\alpha)\) correction. An ingenious perturbative formalism to treat these residual corrections in the independent-particle model of \(\beta\) decay was developed by Jaus and Rasche in the early 1970s.\(^{3,4}\) Originally, these corrections relied heavily on numerical, computer-based calculations.

When experiments on superallowed Fermi transitions reached \(\sim 0.1\%\) accuracy, a sharp discrepancy between low-\(Z\) and high-\(Z\) decays was found.\(^{5,6}\) The first recent theoretical progress occurred when an approximate analytic calculation, supported by general theorems of perturbative quantum field theory, revealed a sharp difference with the older numerical results. Reexamination of \(\mathcal{F}t\) values in light of new \(O(Za^2)\) corrections showed very good agreement with CVC and SM predictions.\(^{1}\) Subsequently, Jaus and Rasche reanalyzed the complicated two-loop calculations, found an error in the older work, and obtained answers completely consistent with the analytical work of Ref. 1.\(^{7}\)

Some relatively small differences remain between Refs. 1 and 7 (referred to as I and II, respectively), concerning the treatment of finite-nuclear-size effects and small \(O(Z^2 a^3)\) corrections. This article provides explicit expressions for finite-nuclear-size effects in terms of realistic nuclear-charge distributions, compares results of I and II, modifies the treatment of \(O(Z^2 a^3)\) corrections, and discusses the effect of these refinements on CVC verification, SM tests, and determination of \(V_{ud}\).

## 2. Classification of \(O(Za^2)\) Corrections

The \(O(Za^2)\) corrections involve the exchange of a photon of momentum \(k\) and a Coulombic photon of momentum \(q\). The diagrams fall into two classes:

- **Class (a)**: The \(k\) photon is interchanged between the decaying nucleons and the positron.
- **Class (b)**: The \(k\) photon is fully attached to the positron (pure QED correction).

The sum of diagrams in each class is ultraviolet and infrared convergent.

Class (a) contributions are further classified according to dependence on the nucleon mass \(M\):
- Terms of leading order in \(M\), called \(Za^2 \Delta_1\) in II, are complicated. Writing the charge form factor of the daughter nucleus as \(G(Q^2) = 1 + G(Q^2) - 1\) (with \(Q^2 \equiv -q^2 \geq 0\)), \(\Delta_1\) splits into:
  - \(\Delta_1^0(E)\): corresponds to a point nucleus, depends on positron energy \(E\).
  - \(\Delta_1^F\): arises from \(G(Q^2) - 1\), is energy independent (neglecting \(O(E/\Lambda)\) terms, where \(\Lambda \equiv \sqrt{6}/a\) and \(a\) is the rms nuclear charge radius), depends on the charge distribution.
- Terms not of leading order in \(M\): constants of first and higher orders in \(\Lambda/M\). Vector and axial-vector contributions are called \(Za^2 \Delta_2\) and \(Za^2 \Delta_3\), respectively.
- Class (b) diagrams are denoted by \(Z\alpha^{2}\Lambda_{4}(E)\) in II.

Analytic expressions for \(\Delta_{1}^{0}(E)\) and \(\Delta_{4}(E)\) obtained in the extreme relativistic approximation (ERA) are given in paper I. In the ERA, the sum is:

\[\Delta_{1}^{0}(E) + \Delta_{4}(E)\equiv \ln (M / m) - \frac{5}{12}\ln (2E / m) + \frac{43}{18}, \quad (1)\]

## 4. Finite-Nuclear-Size Effects

### 4.1 General Expressions

After neglecting terms of \(O(E/\Lambda)\) and performing integrations, \(\Delta_{1}^{F}\) can be written as:

\[\Delta_{1}^{F} = \frac{4}{\pi^{2}}\int_{0}^{1}\left[\frac{u}{1 - u}\right]^{1 / 2}du\int_{0}^{1}dv\int_{0}^{\infty}\frac{dQ}{Q} [G(Q^{2}) - 1]\arctan \left[\frac{Mu^{1 / 2}v}{Q(1 - u)^{1 / 2}}\right]. \quad (2)\]

Evaluating this for a spherically symmetric charge distribution \(\rho(r)\) (normalized such that \(\int \rho(r)d^{3}r = 1\)) yields:

\[\Delta_{1}^{F} = 1 - \gamma -4\pi \int_{0}^{\infty}\rho (r)r^{2}\ln (Mr)d r\] \[-(8 / M)\int_{0}^{\infty}\rho (r)r[1 + \gamma +\ln (Mr)]d r~, \quad (4)\]

where \(\gamma = 0.5772\ldots\), and terms of \(O((\Lambda/M)^{3})\) are neglected.

The contributions from \(\Delta_{2}\) and \(\Delta_{3}\) are:

\[\Delta_{2} = (4 / M)\int_{0}^{\infty}\rho (r)r dr(1 - \pi /4Mr), \quad (5)\]

\[\Delta_{3} = \frac{8g_{A}(1 + \mu\nu)}{M}\int_{0}^{\infty}\rho (r)r\left[\gamma +\ln (Mr) - \frac{1}{2} +\frac{\pi}{8Mr}\right]dr, \quad (6)\]

where \(g_{A} = 1.25\), \(1 + \mu \nu = 4.70\) arises from the isovector magnetic moment of the nucleon, and terms of \(O((\Lambda/M)^3)\) are neglected.

### 4.2 Specific Nuclear Models

**Uniformly charged sphere** of radius \(R = (\frac{2}{3})^{1 / 2}a = 10^{1 / 2} / \Lambda\):

\[\begin{array}{l}{\Delta_{1}^{F} = \ln \left[\frac{\Lambda}{M}\right] - \kappa_{2} - \frac{3}{10^{1 / 2}\pi}\frac{\Lambda}{M}\left[\frac{1}{2} +\gamma +\ln 10^{1 / 2} + \ln \left[\frac{M}{\Lambda}\right]\right],}\\ {\Delta_{2} = \frac{3}{2\times 10^{1 / 2}\pi}\frac{\Lambda}{M}\left[1 - \frac{\pi}{2\times 10^{1 / 2}}\frac{\Lambda}{M}\right],}\\ {\Delta_{3} = \frac{3}{10^{1 / 2}\pi}g_{A}(1 + \mu \nu)\frac{\Lambda}{M}\left[\gamma -1 + \ln 10^{1 / 2} + \ln \left[\frac{M}{\Lambda}\right] + \frac{\pi}{4\times 10^{1 / 2}}\frac{\Lambda}{M}\right],} \end{array} \quad (7)\]

where \(\kappa_{2}\equiv \gamma -\frac{4}{3} +\ln 10^{1 / 2} = 0.395\).

**Modified Gaussian model** \(\rho(r) = \mathrm{const}\times (1 + \alpha k^{2}r^{2} / a^{2})e^{- r^{2}k^{2} / a^{2}}\) with \(\alpha = (Z - 2) / 3\), \(k^{2} = (\frac{3}{2})(2 + 5\alpha) / (2 + 3\alpha)\) (Ref. 8):

\[\begin{array}{l}{\Delta_{1}^{F} = \ln \left[\frac{\Lambda}{M}\right] - \kappa_{1}(Z) - \frac{8}{(6\pi^{3})^{1 / 2}}\frac{k}{2 + 3\alpha}\frac{\Lambda}{M}\left[\left[1 + \frac{\gamma}{2} +\ln \left[\frac{6^{1 / 2}}{k}\frac{M}{\Lambda}\right]\right](1 + \alpha) + \frac{\alpha}{2}\right],}\\ {\Delta_{2} = \frac{4}{(6\pi^{3})^{1 / 2}}\frac{k}{2 + 3\alpha}\frac{\Lambda}{M}\left[1 + \alpha -\left[\frac{\pi^{3}}{96}\right]^{1 / 2}\frac{k}{M}\left[1 + \frac{\alpha}{2}\right]\right],}\\ {\Delta_{3} = \frac{8}{(6\pi^{3})^{1 / 2}}g_{A}(1 + \mu \nu)\frac{k}{2 + 3\alpha}\frac{\Lambda}{M}\left[\left[\frac{\gamma - 1}{2} +\ln \left[\frac{6^{1 / 2}}{k}\frac{M}{\Lambda}\right]\right](1 + \alpha) + \frac{\alpha}{2} +\left[\frac{\pi^{3}}{6}\right]^{1 / 2}\frac{k}{8}\frac{\Lambda}{M}\right],} \end{array} \quad (10)\]

where \(\kappa_{1}(Z)\equiv \frac{1}{2} [\gamma +\ln (3 / 2k^{2}) + 2\alpha /(2 + 3\alpha)]\).

The difference of 1.8 sec with paper II is mainly due to use of realistic nuclear charge distributions.

**Table IV** lists values of \(V_{ud}\) and \(V_{ud}^{2} + V_{us}^{2} + V_{ub}^{2}\) obtained from various combinations of \(\mathcal{F}t\) values, using \(V_{us} = 0.220\pm 0.002\) (Ref. 13):

| Combination | \(V_{ud}\) | \(V_{ud}^{2}+V_{us}^{2}+V_{ub}^{2}\) |
|-------------|---------|-------------------------------|
| \(^{14}\mathrm{O}\) | 0.9739 ± 0.0015 | 0.9969 ± 0.0031 |
| Average of \(^{14}\mathrm{O}\) and \(^{26}\mathrm{Al}^{m}\) | 0.9744 ± 0.0012 | 0.9979 ± 0.0025 |
| Average of 4 low-Z decays | 0.9746 ± 0.0011 | 0.9982 ± 0.0023 |
| Average of 8 decays | 0.9744 ± 0.0010 | 0.9979 ± 0.0021 |

The results are in very good agreement with the third-generation SM at the level of its quantum corrections. Such accurate agreement can be used to derive interesting constraints on new physics.\(^{14}\)

## 8. Dependence on Nuclear Mismatch Corrections \(\delta_c\)

Using the same radiative corrections and errors as in Table III, results based on alternative \(\delta_c\) calculations are:

- **Wilkinson (W)** (Ref. 9): \((\mathcal{F}t)_{\mathrm{ave}} = 3069.8\pm 1.6\) s (\(\chi^{2}/\nu = 0.97\))
- **Ormand and Brown (OB)** (Ref. 15): \((\mathcal{F}t)_{\mathrm{ave}} = 3076.1\pm 1.6\) s (\(\chi^{2}/\nu = 1.01\))
- **Towner-Hardy-Harvey (THH)** (Ref. 12): 3070.6 ± 1.6 s (\(\chi^{2}/\nu = 0.57\)) [Table III]

All fits with the older \(O(Z\alpha^{2})\) corrections were very poor, so the new results are highly significant. The THH and W calculations lead to similar averages, while the OB result differs from THH by +0.18%, implying \(V_{ud} = 0.9735\pm 0.0010\) and \(V_{ud}^{2} + V_{us}^{2} + V_{ub}^{2} = 0.9962 \pm 0.0021\).

### Model-Independent Upper Bound

Assuming all corrections except \(\delta_c\) are well understood and \(\delta_c > 0\), the lowest \(\mathcal{F}t\) value calculated with \(\delta_c = 0\) provides an upper bound. From \(^{26}\mathrm{Al}^{m}\) data, \(\mathcal{F}t \leq 3078.6\pm 1.4\) s. The THH and W \(\delta_c\) values essentially obey this bound, but the central values of \(^{14}\mathrm{O}\), \(^{34}\mathrm{Cl}\), \(^{42}\mathrm{Sc}\), and \(^{46}\mathrm{V}\) \(\mathcal{F}t\) values calculated with OB \(\delta_c\) values are somewhat larger.

### Alternative Determination

Using the \(\mathcal{F}t\) value for \(^{26}\mathrm{Al}^{m}\) calculated with \(\delta_c = (0.34\pm 0.08)\%\) (the average of THH, W, and OB values, with error covering the range) leads to \((\mathcal{F}t)_{\mathrm{Al}} = 3068.1\pm 2.8\) s, corresponding to \(V_{ud} = 0.9748\pm 0.0010\) and \(V_{ud}^{2} + V_{us}^{2} + V_{ub}^{2} = 0.9986 \pm 0.0021\). This is compatible with the Table IV determination.

---

## 9. Discussion and Conclusions

The claim by Drukarev and Strikman\(^{16}\) that screening due to atomic electrons is considerably larger than given by conventional theory\(^{9}\) is noted: inclusion of their additional screening correction would worsen the very good fit of Table III.

The discrepancies mentioned are at the 0.1–0.2% level—accuracies seldom attained in particle physics—and are much smaller than the overall quantum corrections of ∼4%. It is hoped that nuclear and atomic theorists and \(\beta\)-decay experimentalists will reexamine these small corrections in light of the new \(O(Za^{2})\) calculations. Accurate measurements of \(C\) and \(\pi \beta\) decays would be most welcome.
