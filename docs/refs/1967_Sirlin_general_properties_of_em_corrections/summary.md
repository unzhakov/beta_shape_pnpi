# General Properties of the Electromagnetic Corrections to the Beta Decay of a Physical Nucleon

A. Sirlin

*Physical Review*, Volume 164, Number 5, 25 December 1967

Department of Physics, New York University, New York, New York
(Received 26 June 1967)

---

## Abstract

Some general properties of the electromagnetic corrections to the \(\beta\) decay of a physical nucleon (a nucleon in the presence of strong interactions) are discussed. The aim is to isolate and determine general properties independent of the details of strong interactions and assumptions about the existence of the intermediate boson. The method separates out in a finite and gauge-invariant manner all terms of order \(1/k\) in the hadronic covariants, then examines properties of other contributions. Under general and plausible mathematical assumptions, all terms of order \(\alpha\) in the correction factor to the electron spectrum that explicitly depend on the electron energy \(E\) and electron mass \(m\) can be rigorously computed, despite strong interaction complications, provided contributions of relative order \(\alpha q/M\), \(\alpha(E/M)\ln(M/E)\), and \(\alpha \nu_N/c\) are neglected. The electromagnetic correction of order \(\alpha\) to the shape of the allowed electron spectrum is given by a single universal function \(g(E, E_m, m)\), independent of strong interaction details, intermediate boson assumptions, and the ratio \(M_{GT}/M_F\). This function is physically significant, particularly for nuclear \(\beta\) decays with \(E_m/m \gg 1\). Model-dependent terms that contribute to the lifetime are discussed but not evaluated. Other observables for which corrections can be evaluated model-independently are illustrated by the virtual radiative corrections to the longitudinal polarization of the electron in allowed transitions. The paper implicitly assumes the validity of the vector and axial-vector theory of weak interactions.

---

## I. INTRODUCTION

The radiative corrections to \(\beta\) decay of a nucleon have been discussed extensively.\(^{1-7}\) They are of interest for their relevance to universality of weak interactions and because they alter the shape of the allowed electron spectrum in \(\beta\) decay, with significant effects when the end-point energy is large compared to the electron mass.\(^{8}\)

A key theoretical aspect is that these corrections are logarithmically divergent in the \(V-A\) theory when evaluated for a bare nucleon, while the corresponding quantities for muon decay are finite and well defined. Two main approaches to save universality have been suggested:

1. Weak interactions are mediated by an intermediate boson.\(^{9-11}\)
2. Strong interactions themselves may provide a natural ultraviolet cutoff.\(^{12-14}\)

More recently, Bjorken\(^{15}\) and Abers, Norton, and Dicus,\(^{16}\) using current-algebra techniques, have asserted that part of the electromagnetic corrections to the vector decay coupling constant contains a divergent part independent of strong interactions. However, this does not give a complete answer because: (a) there may be contributions from the axial current that are also divergent and model-dependent, and (b) possible intrinsic structure effects of weak interactions from intermediate boson calculations may alter convergence properties.\(^{11}\)

This paper bypasses the convergence problem, taking the hopeful view that the weak, electromagnetic, and strong interactions conspire to give a finite value for the relative renormalization of \(\beta\) and \(\mu\) decay coupling constants. The aim is to isolate and determine general properties of the corrections independent of strong interaction details and intermediate boson assumptions.

## II. THE SEPARATION

The virtual radiative corrections of order \(\alpha\) are separated into two parts. Part one is: (i) independent of strong interaction details; (ii) contains all terms of order \(1/k\) in hadronic covariants; (iii) finite (in the uv region) and gauge-invariant. Part two depends on strong interaction details and involves three- and four-point functions.

### A. Photon Exchange Between Hadronic and Electron Lines

Diagrams where a photon is exchanged between hadronic and electron lines are divided into three classes: photon emitted from an electromagnetic vertex attached to the external proton line, external neutron line, or from internal structure of the weak vertex. The contribution of the first class [Fig. 1(a)] is:\(^{18}\)

\[M^{(p)} = -\frac{G_{\nu}^{0}\alpha}{\sqrt{2}4\pi^{2}i}\int d^{4}k D_{\mu \nu}(k)[\bar{u}_{e}(2l_{r} - \gamma_{r}k)O_{\lambda}\nu], \quad (1)\]

where \(p_1\), \(p_2\), \(l\), and \(k\) are the neutron, proton, electron, and photon four-momenta; \(\lambda_{\min}\) is the photon mass; \(O_{\lambda} \equiv \gamma_{\lambda}(1 + \gamma_{5})\); \(G_{\nu}^{0}\) is the bare coupling constant of the weak-vector current; \(W_{\lambda}\) and \(\bar{\Gamma}_{\mu}^{(p)}\) are the fully dressed renormalized proper vertices; and \(i\bar{S}_{p}^{\prime}\) is the fully dressed renormalized propagator of the proton. The photon propagator is chosen in the Landau gauge:

\[D_{\mu \nu}(k) = \frac{(g_{\mu\nu} - k_{\mu}k_{\nu} / k^2 + i\epsilon)}{k^2 - \lambda_{\min}k^2 + i\epsilon}. \quad (2)\]

The "proton electromagnetic form factor" \(F_{\mu}^{(p)}(p_2, p_2 + k)\) is introduced for the case where \(p_2\) corresponds to an on-shell proton but \(p_2 + k\) is off-shell:

\[\begin{array}{rl} & {\bar{u}_p\bar{\Gamma}_{\mu}^{(p)}(p_2,p_2 + k)\bar{S}_p^{\prime}(p_2 + k)}\\ & {\qquad = \bar{u}_pF_{\mu}^{(p)}(p_2,p_2 + k)[\bar{p}_2 + k - m_p]^{-1},} \end{array} \quad (3)\]

where \(m_p\) is the proton mass. Using the generalized Ward identity:

\[k_{\mu}F_{\mu}^{(p)}(p_{2},p_{2} + k) = k, \quad (4a)\]
\[F_{\mu}^{(p)}(p_{2},p_{2}) = \gamma_{\mu}. \quad (4b)\]

Equations (4) suggest writing:\(^{19}\)

\[F_{\mu}^{(p)}(p_{2},p_{2} + k) = \gamma_{\mu} + X_{\mu}^{(p)}(p_{2},p_{2} + k), \quad (5a)\]

where

\[k_{\mu}X_{\mu}^{(p)}(p_{2},p_{2} + k) = 0, \quad (5b)\]
\[X_{\mu}^{(p)}(p_{2},p_{2}) = 0. \quad (5c)\]

The terms of order \(1/k\) in the hadronic covariant are separated:

\[\begin{array}{rl} & {H_{\mu \lambda}^{(p)}(p_{2},p_{1},k)\equiv \bar{u}_p\bar{\Gamma}_{\mu}^{(p)}(p_{2},p_{2} + k)\bar{S}_p^{\prime}(p_{2} + k)}\\ & {\qquad \times W_{\lambda}(p_{2} + k,p_{1})u_n = (k^2 +2p_{2}\cdot k + i\epsilon)^{-1}}\\ & {\times \bar{u}_p[2(p_{2} + k_\mu)W_\lambda (p_{2},p_{1}) + R_{\mu \lambda}^{(p)}(p_{2},p_{1},k)]u_n,} \end{array} \quad (6a)\]

where

\[R_{\mu \lambda}^{(p)}(p_{2},p_{1},k) = (2p_{2\mu} + k_{\mu})[W_{\lambda}(p_{2} + k,p_{1}) - W_{\lambda}(p_{2},p_{1})] + X_{\mu}^{(p)}(p_{2},p_{2} + k)(p_{2} + k + m_{p})W_{\lambda}(p_{2} + k,p_{1}) + [\gamma_{\mu},k]W_{\lambda}(p_{2} + k,p_{1}) / 2. \quad (6b)\]

Assuming \(\partial W_{\lambda} / \partial k_{\mu}(p_{2} + k,p_{1})|_{k_{\mu} = 0}\) exists, \(R_{\mu \lambda}^{(p)}\to 0\) linearly with \(k_{\mu}\) as \(k_{\mu}\to 0\).

A similar analysis applies to diagrams with photon exchange between the electron and the electromagnetic vertex attached to the external neutron line [Fig. 1(b)]. Because the neutron has zero charge:

\[k_{\mu}F_{\mu}^{(n)}(p_{1} - k,p_{1}) = 0, \quad (7a)\]
\[F_{\mu}^{(n)}(p_{1} - k,p_{1}) = 0. \quad (7b)\]

The matrix element for this contribution has the hadronic covariant:

\[H_{\mu \lambda}^{(n)}(p_{2},p_{1},k) = [k^{2} - 2p_{1}\cdot k + i\epsilon ]^{-1} [\bar{u}_{\mu}W_{\lambda}(p_{2},p_{1} - k)(p_{1} - k + m_{n}) F_{\mu}^{(n)}(p_{1} - k,p_{1})u_{n}]. \quad (7c)\]

\(H_{\mu \lambda}^{(n)}\) is "regular" as \(k_{\mu}\to 0\).

Let \(G_{\mu \lambda}(p_{2},p_{1},k)\) be the matrix element for photon emission from any internal line of the proper vertex of the weak-interaction current [Fig. 1(c)]. Using the generalized Ward identity for electromagnetic vertex functions:\(^{20}\)

\[k_{\mu}G_{\mu \lambda}(p_{2},p_{1},k) = W_{\lambda}(p_{2},p_{1}) - W_{\lambda}(p_{2} + k,p_{1}). \quad (8a)\]

### B. Combined Matrix Element \(M_1\)

The matrix element \(M_1\) (in the Landau gauge) of all diagrams with photon exchange between hadronic and electron lines [Figs. 1(a)-(c)] is:

\[M_{1} = -\frac{G_{\nu}^{0}}{\sqrt{2}}\frac{\alpha}{4\pi^{2}i}\int \frac{d^{4}k D_{n\mu}(k)[\bar{u}_{e}(2l_{r} - \gamma_{r}k)O_{\lambda}\nu_{\tau}]}{(k^{2} - 2l\cdot k + i\epsilon)} \times \left[\bar{u}_{p}\left[\frac{(2p_{2\mu} + k_{\mu})W_{\lambda}(p_{2\mu}p_{1})}{(k^{2} + 2p_{2}\cdot k + i\epsilon)} +T_{\mu \lambda}(p_{2\mu}p_{1}k)\right]u_{n}\right], \quad (9a)\]

where

\[T_{\mu \lambda}(p_{2},p_{1},k) = \frac{R_{\mu\lambda}^{(p)}(p_{2},p_{1},k)}{k^{2} + 2p_{2}\cdot k + i\epsilon} +G_{\mu \lambda}(p_{2},p_{1},k) +\frac{R_{\mu\lambda}^{(n)}(p_{2},p_{1},k)}{k^{2} - 2p_{1}\cdot k + i\epsilon}, \quad (9b)\]
\[R_{\mu \lambda}^{(n)}(p_{2},p_{1},k) = W_{\lambda}(p_{2},p_{1} - k)(p_{1} - k + m_{n}) F_{\mu \lambda}^{(n)}(p_{1} - k,p_{1}). \quad (9c)\]

The tensor-pseudotensor \(T_{\mu \lambda}\) has two basic properties: (a) it is "regular" as \(k\rightarrow 0\); (b) it is transversal:

\[k_{\mu}T_{\mu \lambda} = 0. \quad (10)\]

Equation (10) follows from Eqs. (4a), (7a), (8a) and definitions (9b) and (9c).

Thus, Eq. (9a) achieves a separation of Figs. 1(a)-(c) into two parts: the first part is finite (in the Landau gauge), contains all terms of order \(1/k\) in the hadronic covariant, and can be evaluated independently of strong interaction details; the second part depends on strong interaction details through \(T_{\mu \lambda}\) and is regular as \(k_{\mu}\rightarrow 0\).

### C. Gauge Invariance and Additional Diagrams

To obtain gauge-invariant matrix elements, one must add diagrams where the photon is emitted and absorbed by the electron line (electron wave function renormalization, Fig. 2) and by the hadronic line (Fig. 3).

In the Landau gauge, after mass renormalization, the electron wave function renormalization to order \(\alpha\) is:

\[\begin{array}{l}{M_{2} = -\frac{\alpha}{8\pi^{2}i}\frac{G_{\nu}^{0}}{\sqrt{2}} [\bar{u}_{p}W_{\lambda}(p_{2},p_{1})u_{n}]}\\ {\times \int \frac{d^{4}k D_{n\mu}(k)[\bar{u}_{e}(2l_{r} - \gamma_{r}k)I(2l_{r} - k\gamma_{r})(l + m)O_{\lambda}v_{\tau}]}{(k^{2} - 2l\cdot k + i\epsilon)^{2}2m^{2}}.} \end{array} \quad (11)\]

The integral in Eq. (11) is finite in the uv region and \(M_2\) is proportional to the zeroth-order matrix element:

\[M_{0} = \frac{G_{\nu}^{0}}{\sqrt{2}} [\bar{u}_{e}O_{\lambda}v_{\tau}][\bar{u}_{p}W_{\lambda}(p_{2},p_{1})u_{n}]. \quad (12)\]

The matrix element for diagrams where a photon is emitted and absorbed by the hadronic line (Fig. 3) must have the structure:

\[M_{3} = \frac{\alpha}{2\pi} [\bar{u}_{e}O_{\lambda}v_{\tau}][V_{\lambda} + A_{\lambda}], \quad (13a)\]

where

\[V_{\lambda} = \bar{u}_{p}[f_{1}(q^{2})\gamma_{\lambda} + if_{2}(q^{2})\sigma_{\lambda \nu}q_{\nu} + f_{3}(q^{2})q_{\lambda}]u_{n}, \quad (13b)\]

and \(q\) is the total momentum transfer to the leptons. Neglecting terms proportional to \(\alpha q\) and the \(q^{2}\) dependence of form factors, only \(f_{1}(0)\gamma_{\lambda}\) and \(g_{1}(0)\gamma_{\lambda}\gamma_{5}\) survive.

All infrared terms from Fig. 3 arise from graphs where the photon is emitted and absorbed by the outgoing proton and are contained in the convection-convection contribution:

\[M_{3}^{(c)} = -\frac{\alpha}{8\pi^{2}i} M_{0}\int \frac{d^{4}k D_{\mu \lambda}(k)(2p_{2} - k)_{\mu}(2p_{2} - k)_{\nu}}{(k^{2} - 2p_{2}\cdot k + i\epsilon)^{2}}. \quad (14)\]

The contributions of Eqs. (11), (14), and the first term in Eq. (9a) are each finite in the uv region, and their sum is invariant under gauge transformations of the photon propagator.

### D. Extension to Intermediate Boson Case

The separation and all equations extend immediately to the case of an intermediate boson by: replacing \(W_{\lambda}\) everywhere by \(W_{\mu}D_{\mu \lambda}^{a}\) (where \(D_{\mu \lambda}^{a}\) is the boson propagator), and redefining \(G_{\mu \lambda}\) to include photon emission by the intermediate boson. Graphs with photon exchange between the boson and electron are included in the \(T_{\mu \lambda}\) contribution in Eq. (9a); all other graphs with photon attached to the intermediate boson are included in Eq. (13a).

## III. GENERAL PROPERTIES OF MODEL-DEPENDENT CONTRIBUTIONS

The term involving \(T_{\mu \lambda}\) in Eq. (9a) is discussed. Aside from constant factors, it is given by:

\[I = -\frac{\alpha}{2\pi}\int \frac{d^{4}k[\bar{u}_{e}(2l_{\mu} - \gamma_{\mu}k)O_{\lambda}v_{\nu}][\bar{u}_{p}T_{\mu\lambda}(p_{2},p_{1},k)u_{n}]}{(k^{2} + i\epsilon)(k^{2} - 2l\cdot k + i\epsilon)}, \quad (15)\]

where the transversality relation of Eq. (10) has been used and \(\lambda_{\min} = 0\) because the integral is infrared-convergent.

Consider the contribution \(K\) involving the factor \(\gamma_{\mu}k\) in the leptonic covariant:

\[K = -\frac{\alpha}{2\pi} [\bar{u}_{e}\gamma_{\mu}\gamma_{\rho}O_{\lambda}v_{\nu}]\int \frac{d^{4}k k_{\rho}[\bar{u}_{p}T_{\mu\lambda}(p_{2},p_{1},k)u_{n}]}{(k^{2} + i\epsilon)(k^{2} - 2l\cdot k + i\epsilon)}. \quad (16)\]

Using the separation:

\[[k^{2} - 2l\cdot k + i\epsilon]^{-1} = [k^{2} + i\epsilon]^{-1} + 2l\cdot k[k^{2} + i\epsilon]^{-1}[k^{2} - 2l\cdot k + i\epsilon]^{-1}, \quad (17a)\]

\(K\) can be written as:

\[K = K_{1} + K_{2}, \quad (17b)\]
\[K_{1} = -\frac{\alpha}{2\pi} [\bar{u}_{e}\gamma_{\mu}\gamma_{\rho}O_{\lambda}v_{\nu}]\int \frac{d^{4}k k_{\rho}[\bar{u}_{p}T_{\mu\lambda}(p_{2},p_{1},k)u_{n}]}{(k^{2} + i\epsilon)^{2}}, \quad (17c)\]
\[K_{2} = -\frac{\alpha}{\pi} [\bar{u}_{e}\gamma_{\mu}\gamma_{\rho}O_{\lambda}v_{\nu}]\int \frac{d^{4}k k_{\rho}k_{\sigma}[\bar{u}_{p}T_{\mu\lambda}(p_{2},p_{1},k)u_{n}]}{(k^{2} + i\epsilon)^{2}(k^{2} - 2l\cdot k + i\epsilon)}. \quad (17d)\]

Because \(T_{\mu \lambda}\) is "regular" as \(k_{\mu}\rightarrow 0\), \(K_1\) and \(K_2\) converge in the infrared region.

The integral in Eq. (17c) does not involve the electron four-momentum \(l_{\mu}\) explicitly. If the total momentum transfer \(q\) is neglected in \(T_{\mu \lambda}\) so that \(T_{\mu \lambda}(p_{2},p_{1},k) \approx T_{\mu \lambda}(p_{1},p_{1},k)\), after performing the \(k\) integration \(K_1\) is of the form (see Appendix A):

\[K_{1} = (\alpha /2\pi)[\bar{u}_{e}O_{\lambda}v_{\nu}][\bar{u}_{p}(a\gamma_{\lambda} + b\gamma_{\lambda}\gamma_{5})u_{n}], \quad (17e)\]

where \(a\) and \(b\) are constants independent of \(l_{\mu}\), whose precise values depend on \(T_{\mu \lambda}\) and therefore on strong interaction details and intermediate boson assumptions.

For the contribution \(K_2\), considering the \(R_{\mu \lambda}^{(p)}\) term in \(T_{\mu \lambda}\), one expands:

\[R_{\mu \lambda}^{(p)}(p_{2},p_{1},k) = k_{\alpha}S_{\alpha \mu \lambda}^{(a)}(p_{2},p_{1}) + k_{\alpha}k_{\beta}S_{\alpha \beta \mu \lambda}^{(b)}(p_{2},p_{1},k), \quad (18a)\]

where \(S_{\alpha \mu \lambda}^{(a)}(p_{2},p_{1})\) is independent of \(k\) and \(S_{\alpha \beta \mu \lambda}^{(b)}(p_{2},p_{1},k)\) is regular as \(k\rightarrow 0\). The contribution from \(R_{\mu \lambda}^{(p)}\) is:

\[K_{2}^{(p)} = (\alpha /\pi)l_{\sigma}[\bar{u}_{e}\gamma_{\mu}\gamma_{\rho}O_{\lambda}v_{\nu}] \int \frac{d^{4}k k_{\sigma}k_{\rho}[\bar{u}_{p}(k_{\alpha}S_{\alpha \mu \lambda}^{(a)}(p_{2},p_{1}) + k_{\alpha}k_{\beta}S_{\alpha \beta \mu \lambda}^{(b)}(p_{2},p_{1},k))u_{n}]}{[k^{2} + i\epsilon][k^{2} - 2l\cdot k + i\epsilon][k^{2} + 2p_{2}\cdot k + i\epsilon]}. \quad (18b)\]

The integral involving \(S_{\alpha \mu \lambda}^{(a)}\) contains terms of logarithmic order in \(E\) and \(m\). As it is multiplied by \(l_{\sigma}\), the first term in Eq. (18b) gives contributions of order \(\alpha(E/m_{p})\ln(m_{p}/E)\), \(\alpha(E/m_{p})\), and smaller—"terms of higher order in the lepton four-momentum."

For the term involving \(S_{\alpha \beta \mu \lambda}^{(b)}\), defining:

\[L^{(b)}(\xi) = \int \frac{d^{4}k k_{\sigma}k_{\rho}k_{\alpha}k_{\beta}S_{\alpha \beta \mu \lambda}^{(b)}(p_{2},p_{1},k)}{[k^{2} + i\epsilon][k^{2} - 2\xi l\cdot k + i\epsilon][k^{2} + 2p_{2}\cdot k + i\epsilon]}, \quad (18c)\]

where \(\xi\) is a numerical variable. For \(\xi = 0\), \(L^{(b)}(0)\) is well defined (convergent in the infrared, same uv convergence as \(L^{(b)}(1)\)). Assuming \(L^{(b)}(\xi)\) is continuous in \(-1 \leq \xi \leq 1\) or at least remains bounded, the second term in Eq. (18b) is also of higher order in \(l\), of order \(\alpha E/M\) where \(M\) may be \(m_p\), the intermediate boson mass, or hadron masses characterizing \(S^{(b)}\).

**Conclusion**: The contributions of \(T_{\mu \lambda}\) in Eq. (9a) are of the form (17e), provided terms of order \(\alpha(E/M)\ln(M/E)\) and \(\alpha q/M\) are neglected.

## IV. GENERAL RESULTS

All "model-dependent terms" can be written as:

\[\begin{array}{r}S = \frac{G_{\nu}^{0}}{\sqrt{2}}\frac{\alpha}{2\pi} [\bar{u}_{e}O_{\lambda}v_{\nu}][\bar{u}_{p}(c\gamma_{\lambda} + \rho d\gamma_{\lambda}\gamma_{5})u_{n}],\\ \rho = -G_{A}^{0}/G_{\nu}^{0}, \end{array} \quad (19a)\]

where \(c\) and \(d\) are constants independent of \(E\) and \(m\). Equation (19) contributes to the lifetime corrections. However, neglecting terms of order \(\alpha^{2}\), these terms can be formally "absorbed" in the zeroth-order matrix elements by redefining the vector and axial-vector coupling constants. Therefore, up to terms of order \(\alpha\), Eq. (19) will not alter any observable which to zero order in \(\alpha\) is independent of \(G_{\nu}^{0}\) and \(G_{A}^{0}\). For an unpolarized initial neutron, two such observables are: (i) the electron spectrum when neutrino direction and lepton polarizations are undetected; (ii) the longitudinal polarization of the electron when neutrino direction and polarization are undetected.

### A. Universal Correction Function \(g(E, E_m, m)\)

Combining the first term of Eq. (9a) with Eqs. (11), (14), and (19), and the inner bremsstrahlung diagrams, the decay probability for a neutron (after averaging over initial nucleon polarization, integrating over neutrino direction, and summing over lepton polarizations) is:

\[Pd\rho = \frac{\tilde{\xi}[E_m - E]^{2}p^{2}}{2\pi^{3}} d\rho\left[1 + \frac{\alpha}{2\pi} g(E,E_m,m)\right], \quad (20a)\]

where:

\[\begin{array}{rl} g(E,E_m,m) = & 3\ln \left(\frac{m_p}{m}\right) - \frac{3}{4} + \frac{4}{6}\left[\frac{\tanh^{-1}\beta}{\beta} -1\right] \\ & \times \left[\frac{(E_m - E)}{3E} -\frac{3}{2} +\ln \left[\frac{2(E_m - E)}{m}\right]\right] \\ & + \frac{4}{\beta} L\left(\frac{2\beta}{1 + \beta}\right) \\ & +\frac{1}{\beta}\tanh^{-1}\beta \left[2(1 + \beta^{2}) + \frac{(E_m - E)^{2}}{6E^{2}} -4\tanh^{-1}\beta \right], \end{array} \quad (20b)\]

and:

\[\begin{array}{r}\tilde{\xi} = |G_{V}^{0}|^{2}\left(1 + \frac{\alpha}{\pi}\mathrm{Re}\,c\right)|M_F|^{2} \\ +|G_{A}^{0}|^{2}\left(1 + \frac{\alpha}{\pi}\mathrm{Re}\,d\right)|M_{GT}|^{2}. \end{array} \quad (20c)\]

In these equations, \(E_m\) is the end-point energy, \(p\) is the electron momentum, \(\beta = p/E\), \(M_F\) and \(M_{GT}\) are the Fermi and Gamow-Teller matrix elements, and \(L(x)\) is the Spence function:

\[L(x) = \int_{0}^{x}\frac{dt}{t}\ln(1 - t). \quad (20d)\]

For free neutron decay, \(|M_F|^{2} = 1\), \(|M_{GT}|^{2} = 3\).

The model-dependent constants \(c\) and \(d\) are absorbed in \(\tilde{\xi}\). Equations (20) show that these terms contribute to the lifetime but do not alter the shape of the electron spectrum (up to terms of order \(\alpha\)). The Coulomb term of order \(\alpha\) is not included in Eq. (20a), as it is already incorporated in the Fermi function \(F(Z,E)\).\(^{3}\) Terms of relative order \(\alpha(E/M)\ln(M/E)\), \(\alpha q/M\), and smaller have been neglected.

Equations (20) can be applied to allowed nuclear \(\beta\) decay using an independent particle model of the nucleus.\(^{7}\) The function \(g(E, E_m, m)\) is:

- Independent of strong interaction details and intermediate boson assumptions
- Independent of \(M_{GT}/M_F\)
- The same for electron and positron decay

Thus, \(g(E, E_m, m)\) is a **universal function** describing deviations from the allowed electron spectrum arising from radiative corrections of order \(\alpha\). It differs from previous calculations by a numerical constant independent of \(E\), \(E_m\), and \(m\).\(^{28}\) The corrections to the spectral shape are significant, particularly in allowed nuclear \(\beta\) decays with \(E_m \gg m\).\(^{3}\)

For given \(G_{V}^{0}\) and \(G_{A}^{0}\), the function \(g\) increases the decay probability for neutron \(\beta\) decay by \(1.5\%\) and that of \(^{14}\mathrm{O}\) by \(1.3\%\). This is not a complete lifetime calculation because model-dependent contributions of Eq. (20c) are not considered. However, the effect of \(g\) on the lifetime is rather large as it gives rise to the term \(3(\alpha/2\pi)\ln(m_p/E_m)\), discussed in detail in Ref. 4.

### B. Longitudinal Polarization

Aside from the electron spectrum, there are other observables for which radiative corrections can be computed model-independently, provided terms of order \(\alpha q/M\) and \(\alpha(E/M)\ln(M/E)\) are neglected.\(^{24}\) For the longitudinal polarization of the electron (initial nucleus unpolarized, neutrino direction undetected), the virtual radiative corrections give:\(^{25,26}\)

\[P = -\beta \left[1 + \frac{\alpha}{2\pi} \frac{(1 - \beta^{2})}{\beta}\ln \left(\frac{1 + \beta}{1 - \beta}\right)\right]. \quad (21)\]

This result is valid for an arbitrary value of \(M_{GT}/M_F\). The absence of model-dependent terms \(c\) and \(d\) indicates that Eq. (21) is independent of strong interaction details and intermediate boson assumptions.

## APPENDIX A

In Eq. (17c), writing \(O_{\lambda} \equiv \gamma_{\lambda}(1 + \gamma_{5})\) and using the identity:

\[\gamma_{\mu}\gamma_{\rho}\gamma_{\lambda} = g_{\mu \rho}\gamma_{\lambda} - g_{\mu \lambda}\gamma_{\rho} + g_{\rho \lambda}\gamma_{\mu} - i\epsilon_{\mu \rho \lambda \alpha}\gamma_{\alpha}\gamma_{5}, \quad (A1)\]

with convention \(\epsilon_{0123} = 1\), the contribution of the first term to \(K_1\) is:

\[\frac{\alpha}{2\pi} [\bar{u}_{e}\gamma_{\lambda}(1 + \gamma_{5})v_{\nu}] \int \frac{d^{4}k[\bar{u}_{p}k_{\mu}T_{\mu\lambda}(p_{2},p_{1},k)u_{n}]}{(k^{2} + i\epsilon)^{2}}. \quad (A2)\]

Neglecting the momentum transfer to the leptons \(q = p_1 - p_2\) so that \(T_{\mu \lambda}(p_{2},p_{1},k) \approx T_{\mu \lambda}(p_{1},p_{1},k)\), and noting that after integration only the four-vectors \(p_{2\lambda}\), \(\gamma_{\lambda}\), and \(\gamma_{\lambda}\gamma_{5}\) are available, (A2) reduces to an expression of the form (17e), provided terms of order \(q\) are neglected. Identical conclusions hold for the other contributions of (A1).

## APPENDIX B

Continuity properties of Feynman integrals with reference to Eq. (18c) are discussed. As an illustration, consider the special form:

\[S_{\alpha \beta \mu \lambda}^{(b)} \to g_{\alpha \beta} t_{\mu \lambda} \Lambda^{2} / (\Lambda^{2} - k^{2}), \quad (B1)\]

where \(t_{\mu \lambda}\) is independent of \(k\) and \(\Lambda \gg E\). The integral \(L^{(b)}(\xi)\) is then proportional to:

\[\begin{array}{rl} \mathcal{J}_{3\sigma \tau}(\xi) = & 8i(2\pi)^{-2} \\ & \times \int \frac{d^{4}k k_{\sigma}k_{\tau}[\Lambda^{2} / (\Lambda^{2} - k^{2})]}{[k^{2} + i\epsilon][k^{2} - 2\xi l\cdot k + i\epsilon][k^{2} + 2p_{2}\cdot k + i\epsilon]}. \end{array} \quad (B2)\]

This integral is a continuous function of \(\xi\) in \(-1 \leq \xi \leq 1\). If \(\Lambda \gg m_p\) and terms of order \(m_p/\Lambda\) are neglected:

\[\lim_{\xi \to 0}J_{3\sigma \tau}(\xi) = J_{3\sigma \tau}(0) = \frac{p_{2\sigma}p_{2\tau}}{m_{p}^{2}} - g_{\sigma \tau}\left[\frac{3}{4} + \ln\left(\frac{\Lambda}{m_{p}}\right)\right]. \quad (B3)\]

This agrees with the general discussion after Eq. (18c).

However, \(J_{3\sigma \tau}\) is **not** a continuous function of the four variables \(l_{\mu}\) in the neighborhood of \(l_{\mu} = 0\). The cone \(l^{2} = 0\) ("the light cone of the massless electron") is a surface of logarithmic singularities—these are the mass singularities discussed in Ref. 3 and elsewhere.\(^{27,28}\) In the proton rest frame, one contribution to (B2) can be written approximately as \(-[(l_{\sigma}l_{\tau})/(2m_{p}p)]\ln[(E+p)/(E-p)]\). Replacing \(l_{\sigma} \to \xi l_{\sigma}\), \(p \to \xi p\), \(E \to \xi E\), this is continuous in \(\xi\) and tends to 0 as \(\xi = 0\), but is not continuous in \(E\) and \(p\) as \(E = p\) is a line of logarithmic singularities. By examining continuity with respect to \(\xi\), one avoids the light cone of the massless electron. For the general argument of Sec. III, it is not actually necessary to assume continuity or boundedness—the minimal required property is that such contributions should not diverge faster than \(\ln \xi\) as \(\xi \to 0\).
