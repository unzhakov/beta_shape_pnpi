## I. INTRODUCTION

The \(\mathcal{O}(\alpha)\) radiative correction (RC) to the \(e^{-}(e^{+})\) spectrum has played an important role in precision studies of \(\beta\) decay. It is described by a RC \((\alpha/2\pi)g(E,E_{0})\), where \(E\) and \(E_{0}\) are the energy and end-point energy of the \(e^{-}(e^{+})\) [1,2]. This RC is not contained in Fermi's Coulomb function \(F(Z,E)\), which is separately included. The effect of \((\alpha/2\pi)g(E,E_{0})\) on the spectrum shape is significant, particularly for large values of \(E_{0}\) [1,3]. It also gives important contributions to theoretical calculations of the \(\mathcal{F}t\)-values of superallowed \((0^{+}\rightarrow 0^{+})\) Fermi transitions [4] and the neutron lifetime [5].

The superallowed Fermi transitions play a crucial role in the precise verification of the unitarity of the Cabibbo-Kobayashi-Maskawa (CKM) matrix. The current analysis, which includes large short-distance effects [4], the RC \((\alpha/2\pi)g(E,E_{0})\), and a number of more recent refinements [5,6], leads to the conclusion [7]:

\[|V_{ud}|^2 +|V_{us}|^2 +|V_{ub}|^2 = 0.99995(61), \quad (1)\]

an impressive test of the three-generation standard theory at the level of its quantum corrections. The overall electroweak corrections to Eq. (1) are of \(\mathcal{O}(4\%)\), i.e., 66 times larger than the \(0.061\%\) error.

The correction \((\alpha/2\pi)g\), in conjunction with short-distance effects and refinements discussed in Refs. [4-6], has also been recently applied to the calculation of the muon capture rate in the singlet state of \(\mathrm{H}\) [8].

The aim of this paper is to derive the corresponding RC to the \(\bar{\nu}_{e}(\nu_{e})\) spectrum in allowed \(\beta\) decay, which is of considerable current interest for reactor studies of neutrino oscillations [9-11].

## II. RADIATIVE CORRECTION TO THE \(\bar{\nu}_{e}\) (\(\nu_{e}\)) SPECTRUM

As in Ref. [1], the basic process of neutron \(\beta\) decay, \(n\rightarrow p + e^{-} + \bar{\nu}_{e}\), is considered, neglecting terms of \(\mathcal{O}(\alpha E/M)\) where \(M\) is the nucleon mass.

### A. Virtual Corrections and Zeroth Order Spectrum

The \(\mathcal{O}(\alpha)\) virtual RC, as well as the zeroth order transition probability, are obtained from those in the \(e^{-}\) spectrum by simply replacing \(E\rightarrow \hat{E}, p\rightarrow \hat{p}, \beta\rightarrow \hat{\beta}\), where \(E\), \(p\), and \(m\) are the \(e^{-}\) energy, momentum, and mass, \(\beta = p/E\), \(K\) is the \(\bar{\nu}_{e}\) energy, and \(\hat{E}\), \(\hat{p}\), and \(\hat{\beta}\) are defined by:

\[\hat{E}\equiv E_0 - K;\qquad \hat{p}\equiv (\hat{E}^2 - m^2)^{1/2};\qquad \hat{\beta}\equiv \hat{p}/\hat{E}. \quad (2)\]

The reason is that in deriving the \(\bar{\nu}_{e}\) spectrum, one integrates over the \(e^{-}\) phase space factor \(d^3 p / 2E\) rather than the \(\bar{\nu}_{e}\)'s \(d^3 K / 2K\), with energy conservation expressed by \(\delta(E_0 - K - E)\).

### B. Inner Bremsstrahlung Contributions

The inner bremsstrahlung (IB) contributions present more subtle and theoretically interesting differences with the \(e^{-}\) spectrum.

Calling \(\epsilon_{\gamma}\) the radiated photon energy, the IB amplitude involves two terms: the first behaves as \(1/\epsilon_{\gamma}\) as \(\epsilon_{\gamma}\to 0\); the second is of \(\mathcal{O}(\epsilon_{\gamma}^{0})\). The square of the absolute value of the first term, combined with the \(d^{3}k / 2\epsilon_{\gamma}\) photon phase space factor (\(k\) is the photon momentum), leads to the infrared (IR) divergent contribution. The interference of the two terms and the square of the absolute value of the second are IR convergent and are evaluated separately.

As in [1], the IR divergence is regularized by introducing an infinitesimal photon mass \(\lambda_{\mathrm{min}}\) so that \(\epsilon_{\gamma} = (k^{2} + \lambda_{\mathrm{min}}^{2})^{1/2}\). After integrating over the proton and electron momenta, as well as the \(\bar{\nu}_{e}\) direction, the IR contribution is proportional to \(\int_{0}^{k_{m}}(d^{3}k / 2\epsilon_{\gamma}^{3})f(\epsilon_{\gamma},\nu)\), where:

\[\begin{array}{r}f(\epsilon_{\gamma},\nu) = pE\{(1 / \beta \nu)\ln [(1 + \beta \nu) / (1 - \beta \nu)]\\ -(1 - \beta^{2}) / (1 - \beta^{2}\nu^{2}) - 1\}, \end{array} \quad (3)\]

\(\nu = k / \epsilon_{\gamma}\), \(k_{m} = \hat{E} - m\) is the maximum photon energy for given \(K\), and it is understood that:

\[E = \hat{E} - \epsilon_{\gamma}, \quad (4)\]

which follows from energy conservation.

The complicated dependence of \(f(\epsilon_{\gamma},\nu)\) on \(\epsilon_{\gamma}\) and \(\nu\) hinders the subsequent integration over photon momenta. A convenient strategy is to split \(f(\epsilon_{\gamma},\nu)\) according to:

\[f(\epsilon_{\gamma},\nu) = f(0,\nu) + [f(\epsilon_{\gamma},\nu) - f(0,\nu)]. \quad (5)\]

The IR divergence arises from \(f(0,\nu)\), a function that only involves photon momenta through its dependence on \(\nu\). Changing the integration variable from \(k\) to \(\nu\), the integral \(\int_{0}^{k_{m}}(d^{3}k / 2\epsilon_{\gamma}^{3})f(0,\nu)\) can be performed analytically and leads to a function of \(\hat{\beta}\) and \(\ln(k_{m}/\lambda_{\mathrm{min}})\). Its combination with the virtual \(\mathcal{O}(\alpha)\) RC is well defined since the IR divergences cancel exactly.

The integration of the second term in Eq. (5) is IR convergent, so we set \(\lambda_{\mathrm{min}} = 0\) (\(\nu = 1\)) and replace \(f(\epsilon_{\gamma},\nu) - f(0,\nu) \rightarrow f(k,1) - f(0,1)\). In this latter expression, the \(\epsilon_{\gamma} = k\) dependence is contained in \(E\) and, therefore, also in \(p\), \(\beta\), and \(f(k,1)\) (cf. Equations 3 and 4).

Changing the variable of integration from \(k\) to \(E\) via Eq. (4), the integral \(\int d^{3}k / 2k^{3}[f(k,1) - f(0,1)]\) can also be performed analytically. This includes the integral:

\[I = \int_{m / \hat{E}}^{1}d t\ln (1 - t) / (t^{2} - m^{2} / \hat{E}^{2})^{1/2}, \quad (6)\]

for which a compact analytic representation has been derived:

\[I = -\mathrm{Li}_{2}(2\hat{\beta} /(1 + \hat{\beta})) + \mathrm{tanh}^{-1}\hat{\beta}\ln (2\hat{\beta}^{2} / (1 + \hat{\beta})), \quad (7)\]

where:

\[\mathrm{Li}_{2}(z) = -\int_{0}^{z}(d t / t)\ln (1 - t) \quad (8)\]

is the dilogarithm.

The \(d^{3}k\) integration of the two remaining contributions—the interference of the two terms in the IB amplitude and the absolute value square of the second—are IR convergent. Changing again the variable of integration from \(k\) to \(E\) via Eq. (4), they can also be performed analytically.

### C. Final Expression for \(h(\hat{E},E_0)\)

Combining all the \(\mathcal{O}(\alpha)\) contributions, the radiatively corrected expression for the \(\bar{\nu}_{e}\) spectrum is:

\[dP_{\nu} = dP_{\nu}^{0}[1 + (\alpha /2\pi)h(\hat{E},E_0)], \quad (9)\]

where:

\[dP_{\nu}^{0} = A\hat{p}\hat{E}F(Z,\hat{E})K^{2}dK \quad (10)\]

is the zeroth order spectrum, and:

\[\begin{array}{l}{h(\hat{E},E_0) = 3\ln \left(\frac{m_{p}}{m}\right) + \frac{23}{4} -\frac{8}{\beta}\mathrm{Li}_{2}\left(\frac{2\hat{\beta}}{\mathrm{1} + \hat{\beta}}\right)}\\ {+8\left(\frac{\tanh^{-1}\hat{\beta}}{\hat{\beta}} -1\right)\ln \left(\frac{2\hat{E}\hat{\beta}}{m}\right)}\\ {+4\frac{\tanh^{-1}\hat{\beta}}{\hat{\beta}}\left[\frac{7 + 3\hat{\beta}^{2}}{8} -2\tanh^{-1}\hat{\beta}\right],} \end{array} \quad (11)\]

where \(m_{p}\) is the proton mass. In Eq. (10), \(F(Z,\hat{E})\) is the Fermi Coulomb function evaluated at \(E = \hat{E}\), and \(A\) is a constant independent of \(K\). In neutron \(\beta\) decay, for example, \(A = (g_{V}^{2} + 3g_{A}^{2}) / 2\pi^{3}\), where \(g_{V} = G_{F}V_{ud}\) and \(g_{A}\) are the vector and axial vector coupling constants.

In analogy with the \(e^{-}\) spectrum case [1], Eqs. (9) and (11) can be applied to allowed nuclear \(\beta\) decay using an independent particle model of the nucleus. In that case, \(g_{V}^{2} + 3g_{A}^{2}\) is replaced by \(g_{V}^{2}|M_{F}|^{2} + g_{A}^{2}|M_{\mathrm{GT}}|^{2}\), where \(M_{\mathrm{F}}\) and \(M_{\mathrm{GT}}\) are the Fermi and Gamow-Teller matrix elements. Equation (11) is valid for both \(\bar{\nu}_{e}\) and \(\nu_{e}\) transitions.

Large additional electroweak corrections in the standard theory give very important contributions to the \(\mathcal{F}t\)-values of superallowed Fermi transitions and the neutron lifetime. However, they are constants independent of \(K\) and \(E\), so they do not affect the shape of the \(\bar{\nu}_{e}\) and \(e^{-}\) spectra and are not included in this discussion.

### D. The \(m \rightarrow 0\) Limit

Equation (11) simplifies considerably in the \(m\rightarrow 0\) (\(\hat{\beta}\rightarrow 1\)) limit. Noting that \(\tanh^{-1}\hat{\beta} = \ln[\hat{E}(1+\hat{\beta})/m]\) and \(\mathrm{Li}_{2}(1) = \pi^{2}/6\):

\[\lim_{m\to 0}h(\hat{E},E_0) = 3\ln \left(\frac{m_p}{2E_0}\right) + \frac{23}{4} -\frac{4\pi^2}{3} -3\ln (1 - y), \quad (12)\]

where \(y = K / E_0\). Thus, the \(m\rightarrow 0\) limit of \(h(\hat{E},E_0)\) is **convergent** and has a very simple form.

### E. Comparison with the Electron Spectrum Correction

This is in sharp contrast with the behavior of \(g(E,E_0)\), the function that describes the RC to the \(e^{-}(e^{+})\) spectrum. In the relativistic \(m^{2}\ll E^{2}\) limit:

\[\begin{array}{rl} & {g(E,E_0)}\\ & {\quad \rightarrow 3\ln \left(\frac{m_{p}}{2E_0}\right) - \frac{3}{4} -\frac{2\pi^{2}}{3} +4(\ln x - 1)}\\ & {\quad \times \left[\frac{1 - x}{3x} -\frac{3}{2} +\ln \left(\frac{1 - x}{x}\right)\right] + \frac{(1 - x)^2}{6x^{2}}\ln x}\\ & {\quad +\ln \left(\frac{2E_0}{m}\right)\left[\frac{4(1 - x)}{3x} -3 + \frac{(1 - x)^2}{6x^{2}} +4\ln \left(\frac{1 - x}{x}\right)\right],} \end{array} \quad (13)\]

where \(x = E / E_0\). Equation (13) can be gleaned from the expression between curly brackets in Eq. (4.4) of Ref. [3], after subtracting \(6\ln(\lambda/m_p) + 9/4\) (cf. footnote 23 in Ref. [1]). Thus, the correction to the electron spectrum **diverges logarithmically** in the \(m\rightarrow 0\) limit.

If one multiplies Eq. (13) by \(x^{2}(1-x)^{2}\) (the energy dependence of the zeroth order probability in the \(m\rightarrow 0\) limit) and integrates over \(0\leq x\leq 1\), the cofactor of \(\ln(2E_0/m)\) vanishes, so the correction to the inverse lifetime is indeed free from the electron "mass singularity" [3,12,13].

### F. Theoretical Explanation

The fact that \(h(\hat{E},E_0)\) is convergent in the \(m\rightarrow 0\) limit while \(g(E,E_0)\) is not can be explained theoretically: For given \(K\), in the \(m\rightarrow 0\) limit, all collinear \(e-\gamma\) configurations become energy degenerate with energy \(E_0 - K\) and generally give rise to mass singularities. An elementary but powerful theorem in quantum mechanics leads to the conclusion that these singularities are cancelled in the power series expansions of transition probabilities if the latter are summed over an appropriate ensemble of such degenerate states [13]. In deriving the corrections to the \(\bar{\nu}_{e}(\nu_{e})\) spectrum, the \(d^{3}p\) and \(d^{3}k\) integrations are performed, so indeed one sums over the set of collinear \(e-\gamma\) configurations that become energy degenerate in the \(m\rightarrow 0\) limit. Thus, \(h(\hat{E},E_0)\) should be free of \(\ln m\) singularities, as found. In contrast, this is not the case in the derivation of the electron spectrum, since the \(d^{3}p\) integration is not carried out.

### G. Consistency Check and Illustrative Examples

A useful consistency check: Multiplying Eq. (12) by \(y^{2}(1-y)^{2}\) and Eq. (13) by \(x^{2}(1-x)^{2}\) and integrating over \(x\) and \(y\) from 0 to 1 yields equal results, since both lead to the correction to the inverse lifetime. This has been verified to high accuracy.

As an illustrative example, for \(E_0 = 8\mathrm{MeV}\):

- The RC \((\alpha/2\pi)h(\hat{E},E_0)\) to the \(\bar{\nu}_{e}(\nu_{e})\) spectrum **increases** the transition probability by \(1.17\%\) at \(y = 0.8\) (\(K = 6.4\mathrm{MeV}\)) and by \(0.64\%\) at \(y = 0.2\) (\(K = 1.6\mathrm{MeV}\)). This behavior is reflected in Eq. (12), which shows a positive, monotonically increasing function of \(y\).
- The RC \((\alpha/2\pi)g(E,E_0)\) to the \(e^{-}(e^{+})\) spectrum **decreases** the transition probability by \(1.13\%\) at \(x = 0.8\) (\(E = 6.4\mathrm{MeV}\)) and **increases** it by \(2.90\%\) at \(x = 0.2\) (\(E = 1.6\mathrm{MeV}\)). This can be qualitatively understood by noting that the cofactor of \(\ln(2E_0/m)\) in Eq. (13) is negative for \(x\geq 0.44\) and positive for \(x\leq 0.44\).

Equations (12) and (13) provide very good approximations to the exact expressions when \((E_0-K)^{2}\gg m^{2}\) and \(E^{2}\gg m^{2}\), respectively. As shown in Ref. [1], the \(\mathcal{O}(\alpha)\) RC to the shape of the \(e^{-}(e^{+})\) spectrum is not affected by strong interactions if terms of \(\mathcal{O}(\alpha E/M)\) are neglected; the same conclusion applies to the \(\mathcal{O}(\alpha)\) RC to the \(\bar{\nu}_{e}(\nu_{e})\) spectrum.

## III. RADIATIVE CORRECTION TO THE CONVERSION FROM THE \(e^{-}\) SPECTRUM TO THE \(\bar{\nu}_{e}\) SPECTRUM

Including the \(\mathcal{O}(\alpha)\) RC, the theoretical expressions for the \(e^{-}\) and \(\bar{\nu}_{e}\) spectra in allowed \(\beta\) decay can be cast in the form:

\[\frac{dP_e}{dE} = f_e(E,E_0), \quad (14)\]

\[\frac{dP_\nu}{dK} = f_\nu (K,E_0), \quad (15)\]

where:

\[\begin{array}{r}{f_{e}(E,E_{0}) = A p E(E_{0} - E)^{2}F(Z,E)\Big[1 + \frac{\alpha}{2\pi} g(E,E_{0})\Big],} \end{array} \quad (16)\]

\[\begin{array}{r}{f_{\nu}(K,E_{0}) = A\hat{p}\hat{E} K^{2}F(Z,\hat{E})\Big[1 + \frac{\alpha}{2\pi} h(\hat{E},E_{0})\Big],} \end{array} \quad (17)\]

and \(\hat{E}\) and \(\hat{p}\) are defined in Eq. (2).

Comparing Eqs. (16) and (17) and recalling that \(\hat{E} = E_{0} - K\):

\[\begin{array}{r}{f_{\nu}(K,E_{0}) = f_{e}(\hat{E},E_{0})\Big[1 + \frac{\alpha}{2\pi} h(\hat{E},E_{0})\Big] / } \end{array} \begin{array}{r}{\left[1 + \frac{\alpha}{2\pi} g(\hat{E},E_{0})\right],} \end{array} \quad (18)\]

or, neglecting terms of order \(\mathcal{O}(\alpha^{2})\):

\[\begin{array}{r}{f_{\nu}(K,E_{0}) = f_{e}(\hat{E},E_{0})\Big[1 + \frac{\alpha}{2\pi} (h(\hat{E},E_{0}) - g(\hat{E},E_{0}))\Big].} \end{array} \quad (19)\]

Using Eq. (11) and the exact expression for \(g(E,E_{0})\) (Eq. (20b) of Ref. [1]):

\[\begin{array}{r}{h(\hat{E},E_{0}) - g(\hat{E},E_{0})}\\ {= \frac{13}{2} -\frac{4}{\hat{\beta}}\mathrm{Li}_{2}\Big(\frac{2\hat{\beta}}{1 + \hat{\beta}}\Big) + 4\Big(\frac{\tanh^{-1}\hat{\beta}}{\hat{\beta}} - 1\Big)}\\ {\times \Big[\frac{11}{6} +\ln \Big(\frac{2E_{0}}{m}\Big) + 2\ln \hat{\beta} +2\ln (1 - y) - \ln y - \frac{1}{3(1 - y)}\Big]}\\ {+\frac{\tanh^{-1}\hat{\beta}}{\hat{\beta}}\Big[\frac{3 - \hat{\beta}^{2}}{2} - 4\tanh^{-1}\hat{\beta} -\frac{y^{2}}{6(1 - y)^{2}}\Big],} \end{array} \quad (20)\]

where \(\hat{\beta}\) is defined in Eq. (2) and \(y\equiv K / E_{0}\).

Equations (19) and (20) describe the conversion from the \(e^{-}\) spectrum in a specific decay into the corresponding \(\bar{\nu}_{e}\) spectrum when \(\mathcal{O}(\alpha)\) RC are included.

### Illustrative Example

For \(E_{0} = 8\mathrm{MeV}\), using Eqs. (19) and (20), the RC \((\alpha/2\pi)[h(\hat{E},E_{0}) - g(\hat{E},E_{0})]\):

- **Decreases** \(f_{\nu}(K,E_{0})\) by \(1.74\%\) at \(y = 0.8\) (\(K = 6.4\mathrm{MeV}\))
- **Increases** \(f_{\nu}(K,E_{0})\) by \(1.77\%\) at \(y = 0.2\) (\(K = 1.6\mathrm{MeV}\))

Thus, the correction factor \([1 + (\alpha/2\pi)(h(\hat{E},E_{0}) - g(\hat{E},E_{0}))]\) in Eq. (19) is \(3.57\%\) larger at \(K = 1.6\mathrm{MeV}\) than at \(K = 6.4\mathrm{MeV}\). This behavior reflects the dominant contribution of \(-g(\hat{E},E_{0})\). As a consequence, the RC decreases (increases) \(f_{\nu}(K,E_{0})\) for high (low) \(K\), an effect that becomes sharper as \(E_{0}\) increases.

## IV. CONCLUSIONS

In summary, an analytic expression for the \(\mathcal{O}(\alpha)\) RC to the \(\bar{\nu}_{e}(\nu_{e})\) spectrum in allowed \(\beta\) decay has been derived (Eqs. (9) and (11)). The \(m\rightarrow 0\) limit of this correction is convergent and leads to a very simple expression (Eq. (12)). This is in sharp contrast to the \(\mathcal{O}(\alpha)\) RC to the \(e^{-}(e^{+})\) spectrum, which diverges as \(m\rightarrow 0\), an important difference that has been explained on theoretical grounds. These results have been applied to derive the \(\mathcal{O}(\alpha)\) RC to the conversion from the \(e^{-}\) spectrum to the \(\bar{\nu}_{e}\) spectrum (Eqs. (19) and (20)), a relation that plays an important role in reactor studies of neutrino oscillations [9-11]. A recent study concludes that the conversion procedure can be implemented with an error \(\lesssim 1\%\), provided some conditions are met [10]. If this accuracy is achieved, it is likely that the RC will play a significant role in precision studies of the \(\bar{\nu}_{e}\) spectrum.
